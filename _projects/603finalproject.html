Introduction

Adolescence and young adulthood are key periods of sexual development. Typically starting around high school, many young people in America begin to become sexually active and may engage in risky sexual behaviours such as infrequent condom or birth control use, having sex while intoxicated (drugs/alcohol), or having numerous sexual partners. All of these behaviours can contribute to an increased risk of contracting HIV infection, other sexually-transmitted infections (STIs), unintended pregnancy, and overall consequences to one’s emotional, psychological, and physical safety. Thus, it is essential to determine what risk factors may exist that lead to a heightened likelihood of someone participating in these dangerous behaviours, in the hope that they can be identified early on so that intervention may be more successful.

To that end, numerous studies have found that a history of sexual abuse is associated with a higher frequency of engaging in risky sex behaviours. Houck et al. (2010) found that youth with a history of sexual abuse were significantly more likely to have had sex in the last 3 months and have engaged in unprotected sex. Additionally, when it comes to young women, childhood sexual abuse has been found to increase the odds of experiencing teen pregnancy by 2.21 times (Noll, Shenk, & Putnam, 2009) and to be significantly associated with more sexual partners and an earlier age of first sexual intercourse (Senn, Carey, Vanable, 2008). Significant associations have also been found between childhood sexual abuse in women and sex trading, more sexual partners, and an earlier age at first sexual intercourse (Negriff et al., 2015).

However, virtually all of the research that analyzes this relationship fails to investigate how gender differences may play a role, focusing instead solely on the effect among females. Research that has focussed specifically on male participants, although scant, has found that the relationship is still very significant. A 2012 meta-analysis found that sexually abused boys were significantly more likely to report undisclosed sexual intercourse, multiple sexual partners, and involvement in unplanned pregnancy than boys without a history of sexual abuse (Homma et al.). When directly comparing males and females, Negriff et al. (2015) determined that abused males reported a significantly higher number of lifetime sexual partners than abused females but were less likely to have ever gotten someone pregnant than abused females were to have been pregnant. Furthermore, males on average tend to be at a younger age when they first have sex than females (Abma & Martinez, 2023).

This analysis hopes to contribute to this research by using a nationally-representative sample of American high schoolers to not only confirm whether a significant relationship exists between history of sexual abuse and the frequency of risky sex behaviours, but also to investigate what gender differences exist in this relationship, particularly as to the type of sexual abuse reported and its association with specific sexual risk behaviours by gender and between genders.

Description

The yrbs dataset comes from the Youth Risk Behaviour Surveillance System (YRBSS), a set of surveys run by the Centers for Disease Controla and Prevention (CDC) that track behaviours and experiences that lead to poor health in American students from grades 9 through 12. The surveys are administered every other year; since 1991 through 2021, the YRBSS has collected data from more than 5 million high school students in more than 2,200 separate surveys.

The YRBSS monitors six categories of priority health-risk behaviours and experiences among students; however for this analysis, specific attention has been paid to the categories involving experiences with sexual violence and risky sex behaviours. The data were collected from multiple sources, including a national school-based survey conducted by CDC as well as school-based state, territorial, tribal, and large urban school district surveys conducted by education and health agencies.

Research Question

  • What is the relationship between history of sexual abuse and the frequency of risky sex behaviours in a nationally-representative sample of American high school students?

As stated previously, my main research goal is to answer this research question using a sample of 2,453 sexually active American high schoolers from the CDC’s YRBSS.

  1. What gender differences exist, if any, between male and female students when it comes to the relationship between history of sexual abuse and risky sex behaviours? Is the relationship found to be more or less strong depending on the gender of the student?

To answer this question, a sexual abuse by gender interaction was added to test whether any of the outcomes differed for sexually abused males versus comparison males, for sexually abused females versus comparison females, or for abused males versus abused females.

Hypotheses

Given the results found by past research explained above, this analysis intends to test the following hypotheses:

  1. For Research Question #1: Students with less experience with sexual abuse will reported a lower frequency in performing risky sex behaviours, with students reporting more experience reporting a higher frequency of these behaviours.

This hypothesis will be tested mainly by comparing participants’ sexual abuse scores with their respective risky sex behaviour scores (more information on these scores is below). However, this analysis will also look at specific types of sexual abuse experiences and risky sex behaviours in order to get a clearer and fuller picture of what kinds of abuse significantly contribute to certain behaviours.

  1. For Research Question #2: A significant overall relationship between sexual abuse history and risky sexual behaviours will be found for both male and female students. However, the relationship will be stronger for female students than male students. Additionally, gender differences will exist among the associations between specific type of abuse reported and certain risk behaviours. Specifically, compared to female participants, on average:

    a) Male students will report lower rates of sexual abuse

    b) Male students will report a younger age at first sexual intercourse

    c) Male students will report a higher number of lifetime and recent sexual partners

Variables of Interest

Independent variable

The main independent variable is sexual abuse, which is overall quantified using an abuse score, a summed score of the following variables measuring different aspects of sexual abuse for each participant: (1) lifetime history of forced sexual intercourse, (2) number of experiences of sexual violence over past 12 months, and (3) number of experiences of sexual dating violence over past 12 months. All of these variables are either binary ordinal (0 = no history, 1 = history) or represent the number of abuse experienced.

The abuse score (abuse.sum) has a range of 0, meaning 0 sexual abuse experienced, to 9, meaning maximum abuse experienced, with a higher score indicating more experiences of abuse. A student with a score of 9, for instance, means they had a lifetime history of forced sexual intercourse (+1) and, over the past 12 months, experienced sexual violence 6 or time times (+4) as well as sexual violence from someone they were dating or going out with 6 or more times (+4).

A binary abuse status variable was also calculated for each participant with consideration to their abuse score, with 0 equalling no sexual abuse ever experienced and 1 equalling sexual abuse experienced (to some degree). This status variable was created as a way to measure sexual abuse as a dualistic concept.

Notably, reporting experience with both sexual violence in general and sexual violence from someone they were dating or going out with are not mutually exclusive; that is to say, a student who experienced sexual violence from someone they were dating 4 times in the past 12 months, for instance, could also report that they experienced sexual violence in general (from anyone) 4 times in the past 12 months.

More information about the independent variables that make up the abuse score is below.

Dependent variable

The main dependent variable is risky sex behaviour, which is overall quantified using a risky sex behaviour score, which is a summed score for each participant of the following variables that represent different types of risky sex behaviours: (1) age at first sexual intercourse, (2) number of lifetime sexual partners, (3) number of sexual partners over past 3 months, (4) contraceptive usage (condom or other form of birth control) during last intercourse, (5) use of drugs or alcohol during last intercourse.

The risky sex behaviour score, represents as the variable “risky.sex”, has a range of 2, meaning the least amount of risky sex behaviours, to 21, meaning the most amount of behaviours. A participant with a score of 2 means that they reported being 17 years or older when they first had sexual intercourse and having only 1 sexual partner in their lifetime, with all other behaviours measured at 0. In contrast, a participant with a risky sex behaviour score of 21 means they reported the following: (1) age of 11 or younger at first sexual intercourse (+7), (2) 6 or more sexual partners in their lifetime (+6), (3) 6 or more sexual partners in the past three months (+6), (4) no contraceptive use during last sexual intercourse (+1), and (5) use of drugs or alcohol during last sexual intercourse (+1).

More information about the dependent variables that make up the risky sex behaviour score is below.

Mediator variable

The gender of the student is used as a secondary independent variable as an interaction term between the effect of history of sexual abuse on the reported frequency of risky sex behaviors. Male students were coded as 1 and female students were coded as 0.

risky sex behaviours

  • A new ordinal variable, age.sex, was recoded using sex.13 to allow age at first sexual intercourse for the purposes of calculating a risky sex behaviour score. That is to say, the age.sex variable was the variable used for calculating the risky sex behaviour score, not the original sex.13 variable. The age.sex variable was coded such that a higher value indicates a younger age at first intercourse, and thus, more risky sex behaviour, with the following values: 7 = 11 years or younger, 6 = 12 years old, 5 = 13 years old, 4 = 14 years old, 3 = 15 years old, 2 = 16 years old, 1 = 17 years or older.

contraception = whether participant used a form of contraceptive (condom, birth control pill, IUD, etc.) during last sexual intercourse, coded from original birth control and condom use survey questions (0 = no, did NOT use any contraception, 1 = yes, DID use a form of contraception)

drugs.sex = under influence of alcohol or drugs during last sexual intercourse (0 = no, was NOT under the influence, 1 = yes, WAS under the influence)

Subsetting the Data

The original YRBSS dataset had a total of 242 variables and 17,323 observations. This original dataset, yrbs, whcih contained all data that had been collected as far back as 1991 when the survey first started, was subsetted to only include data collected in the year 2021. Additionally, all columns that were not of direct interest to the present analysis were removed. In accordance with previous research such as those conducted by both Niu et al. (2021) and Negriff et al. (2015), the sample was restricted to only include sexually-active participants.

The resulting final dataset used for analysis, yrbs2021x, has a total of 2,453 rows and 19 columns, with each row representing a single student (N = 2,453).

## Introduction

Adolescence and young adulthood are key periods of sexual development. Typically starting around high school, many young people in America begin to become sexually active and may engage in risky sexual behaviours such as infrequent condom or birth control use, having sex while intoxicated (drugs/alcohol), or having numerous sexual partners. All of these behaviours can contribute to an increased risk of contracting HIV infection, other sexually-transmitted infections (STIs), unintended pregnancy, and overall consequences to one's emotional, psychological, and physical safety. Thus, it is essential to determine what risk factors may exist that lead to a heightened likelihood of someone participating in these dangerous behaviours, in the hope that they can be identified early on so that intervention may be more successful.

To that end, numerous studies have found that a history of sexual abuse is associated with a higher frequency of engaging in risky sex behaviours. Houck et al. (2010) found that youth with a history of sexual abuse were significantly more likely to have had sex in the last 3 months and have engaged in unprotected sex. Additionally, when it comes to young women, childhood sexual abuse has been found to increase the odds of experiencing teen pregnancy by 2.21 times (Noll, Shenk, & Putnam, 2009) and to be significantly associated with more sexual partners and an earlier age of first sexual intercourse (Senn, Carey, Vanable, 2008). Significant associations have also been found between childhood sexual abuse in women and sex trading, more sexual partners, and an earlier age at first sexual intercourse (Negriff et al., 2015).

However, virtually all of the research that analyzes this relationship fails to investigate how gender differences may play a role, focusing instead solely on the effect among females. Research that has focussed specifically on male participants, although scant, has found that the relationship is still very significant. A 2012 meta-analysis found that sexually abused boys were significantly more likely to report undisclosed sexual intercourse, multiple sexual partners, and involvement in unplanned pregnancy than boys without a history of sexual abuse (Homma et al.). When directly comparing males and females, Negriff et al. (2015) determined that abused males reported a significantly higher number of lifetime sexual partners than abused females but were less likely to have ever gotten someone pregnant than abused females were to have been pregnant. Furthermore, males on average tend to be at a younger age when they first have sex than females (Abma & Martinez, 2023).

This analysis hopes to contribute to this research by using a nationally-representative sample of American high schoolers to not only confirm whether a significant relationship exists between history of sexual abuse and the frequency of risky sex behaviours, but also to investigate what gender differences exist in this relationship, particularly as to the type of sexual abuse reported and its association with specific sexual risk behaviours by gender and between genders.

## Description

The yrbs dataset comes from the Youth Risk Behaviour Surveillance System (YRBSS), a set of surveys run by the Centers for Disease Controla and Prevention (CDC) that track behaviours and experiences that lead to poor health in American students from grades 9 through 12. The surveys are administered every other year; since 1991 through 2021, the YRBSS has collected data from more than 5 million high school students in more than 2,200 separate surveys.

The YRBSS monitors six categories of priority health-risk behaviours and experiences among students; however for this analysis, specific attention has been paid to the categories involving experiences with sexual violence and risky sex behaviours. The data were collected from multiple sources, including a national school-based survey conducted by CDC as well as school-based state, territorial, tribal, and large urban school district surveys conducted by education and health agencies.

## Research Question

-   What is the relationship between history of sexual abuse and the frequency of risky sex behaviours in a nationally-representative sample of American high school students?

As stated previously, my main research goal is to answer this research question using a sample of 2,453 sexually active American high schoolers from the CDC's YRBSS.

2.  What gender differences exist, if any, between male and female students when it comes to the relationship between history of sexual abuse and risky sex behaviours? Is the relationship found to be more or less strong depending on the gender of the student?

To answer this question, a sexual abuse by gender interaction was added to test whether any of the outcomes differed for sexually abused males versus comparison males, for sexually abused females versus comparison females, or for abused males versus abused females.

## Hypotheses

Given the results found by past research explained above, this analysis intends to test the following hypotheses:

1.  For Research Question #1: Students with less experience with sexual abuse will reported a lower frequency in performing risky sex behaviours, with students reporting more experience reporting a higher frequency of these behaviours.

This hypothesis will be tested mainly by comparing participants' sexual abuse scores with their respective risky sex behaviour scores (more information on these scores is below). However, this analysis will also look at specific types of sexual abuse experiences and risky sex behaviours in order to get a clearer and fuller picture of what kinds of abuse significantly contribute to certain behaviours.

1.  For Research Question #2: A significant overall relationship between sexual abuse history and risky sexual behaviours will be found for both male and female students. However, the relationship will be stronger for female students than male students. Additionally, gender differences will exist among the associations between specific type of abuse reported and certain risk behaviours. Specifically, compared to female participants, on average:

    a\) Male students will report lower rates of sexual abuse

    b\) Male students will report a younger age at first sexual intercourse

    c\) Male students will report a higher number of lifetime and recent sexual partners

## **Variables of Interest**

### Independent variable

The main independent variable is sexual abuse, which is overall quantified using an abuse score, a summed score of the following variables measuring different aspects of sexual abuse for each participant: (1) lifetime history of forced sexual intercourse, (2) number of experiences of sexual violence over past 12 months, and (3) number of experiences of sexual dating violence over past 12 months. All of these variables are either binary ordinal (0 = no history, 1 = history) or represent the number of abuse experienced.

The abuse score (abuse.sum) has a range of 0, meaning 0 sexual abuse experienced, to 9, meaning maximum abuse experienced, with a higher score indicating more experiences of abuse. A student with a score of 9, for instance, means they had a lifetime history of forced sexual intercourse (+1) and, over the past 12 months, experienced sexual violence 6 or time times (+4) as well as sexual violence from someone they were dating or going out with 6 or more times (+4).

A binary abuse status variable was also calculated for each participant with consideration to their abuse score, with 0 equalling no sexual abuse ever experienced and 1 equalling sexual abuse experienced (to some degree). This status variable was created as a way to measure sexual abuse as a dualistic concept.

Notably, reporting experience with both sexual violence in general and sexual violence from someone they were dating or going out with are not mutually exclusive; that is to say, a student who experienced sexual violence from someone they were dating 4 times in the past 12 months, for instance, could also report that they experienced sexual violence in general (from anyone) 4 times in the past 12 months.

More information about the independent variables that make up the abuse score is below.

### Dependent variable

The main dependent variable is risky sex behaviour, which is overall quantified using a risky sex behaviour score, which is a summed score for each participant of the following variables that represent different types of risky sex behaviours: (1) age at first sexual intercourse, (2) number of lifetime sexual partners, (3) number of sexual partners over past 3 months, (4) contraceptive usage (condom or other form of birth control) during last intercourse, (5) use of drugs or alcohol during last intercourse.

The risky sex behaviour score, represents as the variable "risky.sex", has a range of 2, meaning the least amount of risky sex behaviours, to 21, meaning the most amount of behaviours. A participant with a score of 2 means that they reported being 17 years or older when they first had sexual intercourse and having only 1 sexual partner in their lifetime, with all other behaviours measured at 0. In contrast, a participant with a risky sex behaviour score of 21 means they reported the following: (1) age of 11 or younger at first sexual intercourse (+7), (2) 6 or more sexual partners in their lifetime (+6), (3) 6 or more sexual partners in the past three months (+6), (4) no contraceptive use during last sexual intercourse (+1), and (5) use of drugs or alcohol during last sexual intercourse (+1).

More information about the dependent variables that make up the risky sex behaviour score is below.
